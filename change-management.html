Introduction

Leading a people
and work transformation

AI adoption is accelerating — and so is the pressure to prove impact. Leaders want speed. Employees want clarity and stability. Meanwhile, the technology is evolving faster than most operating rhythms can absorb.

For HR teams responding to this pressure, AI transformation can look like a typical rollout: identify a use case, find a tool, implement and hope it sticks. But the teams that succeed treat AI as a people-and-work transformation, not just a technology upgrade. They build trust, capability, and the habits that change daily work.

"The goal isn't to make your workforce 'use AI.' The goal is to help your organization evolve into new ways of working where AI is embedded, outcomes improve, and people remain at the center of how value is created."

— Alicia Miller, Lead Contributor

This guide is not a technical manual, a vendor comparison, or a vision piece. It is a practical, human-centered guide for enterprise and people leaders to lead the people side of AI transformation.

Chapter 1

How AI transformation
is different

AI adoption isn't like previous technology rollouts. Seven fundamental differences make it harder — and demand a different approach to change management.

01

Probabilistic vs. Deterministic

Traditional software produces consistent, repeatable results. AI models produce outcomes that vary by user and change over time — trained on inherently incomplete data.

02

Continuous Feedback Loops

AI models can be built with ongoing learning mechanisms, resulting in systems that improve — or degrade — over time. They require ongoing monitoring and evaluation.

03

Opaque Intelligence & Trust

AI often feels like a "black box." These systems must build trust before adoption, particularly in regulated or risk-averse cultures with high accuracy thresholds.

04

Choice & Experimentation

Users aren't presented with a single solution — they face a multitude of options and must determine the best one. Experimentation is no longer optional.

05

Continual Learning at the Frontier

Models are continuously refined. Users must explore, experiment, and adapt continuously — not simply learn a fixed set of features once.

06

Blurring Role Boundaries

AI is now co-creating and generating — not just executing tasks. Organizations must revisit job architectures and answer: what is the role of human vs. machine?

07

Psychological & Identity Threats

Technological innovation shifts the demand for skills. People may feel their professional value is diminishing as increasing aspects of their work are handled by technology. This barrier is often underestimated.

Chapter 2

Is your culture
AI-enabling?

The success of AI in the workplace depends not just on technical readiness — but on people and cultural readiness. Adopting AI requires employees to learn new ways of working with technology that isn't fixed, but evolving.

5 Cultural Factors That Drive AI Adoption

01

Experimentation Climate

Organizations must embrace test-and-learn approaches. The optimal design is determined through comparative trials — not top-down mandates.

02

Continuous Learning

Employees must expect ongoing evolution of tools and maintain the motivation to learn — and re-learn — how to optimize with AI. One-time training is not enough.

03

Growth Mindset

Leaders and teams must embrace iteration and unsuccessful outcomes as part of the journey. AI can feel overwhelming — employees need to believe they can adapt.

04

Collaboration & Knowledge Sharing

AI output quality varies by how employees interact with it. Cultivating networks and sharing best practices is critical to unlocking value at scale.

05

Psychological Safety

Layoff announcements combined with AI automation news fuel resistance. Organizations that help employees feel secure will unlock greater adoption and knowledge sharing.

How to build it

Habituate Learning

Schedule regular "AI hours" or workshops where employees explore tools or share successes and challenges.

Reward Curiosity

Recognize individuals who try novel prompts, build new workflows, or contribute to an internal AI playbook.

Normalize Failure

Celebrate what experiments teach — even when they don't pan out — as part of quarterly reviews and town halls.

Leadership Modeling

Have managers and senior leaders share and communicate their own AI usage and exploration openly.

Chapter 3

The COM Model
for AI Adoption

AI is transforming the tasks, decisions, and collaborations that make up everyday workflows. But this shift requires more than access to technology. It demands a deliberate effort to build three levers of behavioral change.

C

Capability

  • Structured training portfolios
  • Role-based learning paths
  • Hands-on workshops & sprints
  • Knowledge-sharing networks
O

Opportunity

  • Dedicated experimentation teams
  • Protected time allocations
  • Open sandboxes & prompt libraries
  • Leadership accountability for access
M

Motivation

  • Connect AI to individual growth
  • Address job fears directly
  • Surface and share success stories
  • Provide focused starting points

Chapter 3 · C

Building employee
capabilities

Increasing AI aptitude requires nuanced approaches — traditional L&D adapted for an evolving landscape. Your learning & development roadmap should address AI as a portfolio, not a single training event.

01

Structured Training

Develop a roadmap with sequenced steps. Know when to curate external content and when to develop in-house.

02

Role-Based Learning Paths

Offer tiered training matched to different roles — AI developer, AI user, manager, senior leader — and different departments.

03

On-Demand Learning

Provide tutorials and recorded demos — and ensure employees know how to find and navigate them efficiently.

04

Hands-On Experience

Orchestrate workshops, AI sprints, and hackathons. Use sprints to test different approaches, measure output quality, and document findings.

05

Knowledge Sharing

Leverage Centers of Excellence, Communities of Practice, and dedicated channels to build peer engagement and real-time problem-solving.

"Don't wait for the playbook. Build it. Best practices for specific contexts are still being discovered — invite employees to contribute new insights and curate a living AI playbook."

Chapter 3 · O

Creating opportunity
for AI usage

Even when motivated and capable, employees often lack the time or space to explore AI. The most experienced people — the ones whose judgment matters most — are frequently the busiest. Without dedicated opportunity, adoption stagnates.

01

Dedicated Experimentation Teams

Assign groups whose primary role is to explore, test, and codify best practices. These teams accelerate ROI and scale adoption by delivering templates that business teams can adopt.

02

Protected Time Allocations

Give employees "AI Fridays" or set exploration quotas — time explicitly protected for experimentation, not squeezed in around other priorities.

03

Resource Access

Provide open sandboxes, prompt libraries, and access to enterprise AI tools without bureaucratic barriers.

04

Leadership Accountability

Ensure staff have the time and resources to succeed. The process of getting from zero to efficiency requires extra time up front — leaders must protect it.

Chapter 3 · M

Sustaining motivation
for AI usage

Motivation grows when purpose is clear, when individuals see how AI connects to their goals, and when fears are addressed directly — not ignored or minimized.

01

Connect to Individual Growth

Help employees answer "What's in it for me?" with clear articulation of career benefits and opportunities unlocked by upskilling.

02

Address Fears Directly

One of the most unique barriers to AI adoption is the fear of automation. Authentic, transparent communication about job protection matters more than actual intentions.

03

Focus Efforts

The pace of change can feel overwhelming. Help employees start by communicating where to begin and providing clear, tangible directions.

04

Show Success Stories

Skeptics abound. For many, seeing it work is what makes them believe it can. Make success visible and specific.

Chapter 4

From pilot
to scale

Proofs of concept and pilots can be essential to de-risking AI adoption, building organizational confidence, and generating the evidence needed to scale. When done correctly, they test more than technical feasibility.

Proof of Concept (POC)

Primary question: "Can it work technically?"

  • Enable innovation in new areas
  • Confirm technical feasibility
  • Identify additional infrastructure requirements
  • Build the initial business case
  • Assess vendor compatibility

Pilot

Primary question: "Will it work in operations and deliver value?"

  • Ensure more robust products and workflows
  • Reduce financial and reputational risk
  • Build buy-in and identify champions
  • Assess change readiness and adoption barriers
  • Evaluate real-world business value

Scaling is not simply replicating the pilot. It requires strengthening processes and ensuring the AI solution is robust enough for larger, more complex environments. Decision criteria for incremental vs. full-scale deployment include geographic variation, support resource capacity, and cost controls.

Conclusion

Momentum
is the work

AI adoption doesn't fail because organizations lack ambition or access to technology. It fails because leaders underestimate how fundamentally different this shift is from what came before. AI is not a system you install and stabilize. It is a capability you introduce into a living organization — one that learns, adapts, and reacts alongside the people using it.

The organizations that move forward are not the ones racing to automate the most work. They are the ones building confidence, capability, and clarity as they go. They treat adoption as a process, not an event. They create feedback loops between technology, people, and outcomes.

There is no finish line for AI transformation. The work is ongoing by design. Momentum is not a phase you reach — it is the result of deliberate choices made over time about how technology and people move forward together.
